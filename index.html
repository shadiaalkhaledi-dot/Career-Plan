1 · Registration

Where does my registration stand?

My situation is specific: I hold a pre-professional Bachelor of Architecture rather than a NAAB-accredited professional degree, and I'm based in Alberta. The most direct route to registration is to license through the US first, then bring the licence home. That makes it a two-part process, and the two parts run in parallel.

Part 1 · Register in Washington

Through NCARB. I already have the experience hours required; the remaining step is passing the ARE exams.

Part 2 · NCARB Certificate

The credential that lets me bring the licence to Alberta. Because my degree is a pre-professional BA, it runs through NCARB's Education Alternative, which requires double the hours in each of the six experience areas (7,480 total).

The key point

These run together. While I study for and pass my exams, I'm banking the hours that satisfy Part 2. The same work moves both forward.

John's question: do the hours need to be earned in Alberta?

No. The updated US-Canada reciprocity agreement (effective January 2026) removed the residency and place-of-practice requirements, so hours earned in the US transfer. (Worth confirming the detail directly with AAA, but that is the current rule.)

Recent NCARB changes work in my favour

  • January 2026: the US-Canada agreement removed the old residency and hours barriers, now accepts my Education Alternative route, and made the Alberta move more direct.
  • November 2025: AXP reporting updated, so older experience still counts (75%, no age cap). Nothing I've already logged is lost.

2 · The role

What's the role, and why an architect?

BIM coordination and project coordination, carried from an architect's point of view. Why an architect: it's who I am, it's the track I'm on through registration, and it fits the career path the work leads to.

What I already do

  • Implemented Revizto efficiently on the aviation work and pushed its adoption forward (building on what the DT team introduced), and presented on it at our BIM Café.
  • Run the coordination tracking across the work.
  • Provide the BIM coordination on the Concourse B packages, and hold the model and coordination standard on Centralized / PBS.

A consistent BIM foundation for the aviation team

As the team grows, someone has to own the standard across every project, not one model at a time:

  • Consistent Revit setups and the right shared families across projects, instead of per-project shortcuts that cost more downstream.
  • Clean models coordinated with consultants: BIM Execution Plans, clash detection, the full discipline of it.
  • People mentored into that standard, so quality isn't dependent on any one person.

I already do this across the Concourse B packages and Centralized / PBS. I want to own it across the aviation work.

3 · What I'd need

What would I need to do it well?

Three things, none of them a big lift.

  1. One person to bring up on Centralized.

    I'm currently the only one modeling it, a single point of failure. The right fit is a strong technologist who'd first learn the project and design history, the model and file setup, and the construction-phase change process, onboarded by shadowing and then a defined mini-scope under my review. Training them, with me guiding, de-risks the project and lets me lead and mentor instead of producing everything myself.

  2. A plan to get me the hours I still need.

    Some protected time and deliberate assignment to the two areas where my remaining NCARB hours live: Programming & Analysis and Project Planning & Design (design-phase and pre-design work). That way my licence relocation and the projects' needs move together. Exam study I'll do on my own time.

  3. A true picture of the load.

    I'll start logging overtime so the real capacity of the work is visible. Not to change deadlines, just so the load is understood and resourced rather than absorbed silently.
